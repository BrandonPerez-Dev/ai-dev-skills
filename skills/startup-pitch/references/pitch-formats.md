# Pitch Formats Reference

All short-form and medium-form pitch templates. Load this file when drafting specific formats in Phase 2-3.

---

## 1. Two-Sentence Pitch (YC / Michael Seibel)

The foundation everything else builds from. YC coaches every company to perfect this first.

**Structure:**
```
Sentence 1: What does your product do? (company name + action + who)
Sentence 2: Walk through the user's specific action or journey.
```

**Rules:**
- Start with the company name and what it does — don't lead with the problem
- Walk through the user path — demonstrates the solution without requiring the audience to fill in blanks
- Eliminate ALL jargon, acronyms, and marketing language
- "Make it sound dumber than you think it should" — Seibel

**Testing:** Email to a smart friend unfamiliar with your space. Ask them to rephrase it back. If they ask clarifying questions, the pitch needs work.

**When to use:** Opening of every investor meeting, cold email first paragraph, any time someone asks "what do you do?"

## 2. One-Liner (3 Variants)

Single-sentence pitch using a formula. The most common shorthand in VC and business conversations.

### Variant A — The "We Help" Formula
```
We help [specific audience] [achieve outcome] by [mechanism].
```
Example: "We help plumbing companies eliminate back-office admin by automating scheduling, invoicing, and customer communication with AI."

### Variant B — Founder Institute Template
```
[Company] is developing [offering] to help [audience] [solve problem] with [secret sauce].
```
Example: "FieldAI is developing an AI operations platform to help trades businesses eliminate back-office overhead with autonomous scheduling, billing, and customer communication."

### Variant C — Steve Blank XYZ
```
We help [X] do [Y] by doing [Z].
```
Example: "We help trades businesses run without a back-office admin by handling their calls, texts, scheduling, and invoicing with AI."

**Generate all three variants.** The user picks which resonates most, or blends elements. Different contexts favor different variants.

## 3. Tagline / 10-Second Pitch

The absolute shortest description. Designed so "a very smart ten-year-old would understand what you do."

**Specs:**
- Maximum 80 characters (~3-7 words beyond company name)
- No jargon, no metrics, no acronyms
- Should be "like baby food — predigested"

**Template:**
```
[Company] — [3-7 simple words].
```

**Examples from funded companies:**
- "Book rooms with locals, rather than hotels" (Airbnb)
- "Banking stack for startups" (Mercury)
- "Making gardening ridiculously easy" (Seedsheet)

**When to use:** Email footers, name badges, LinkedIn headline, networking openers, anywhere you have 10 seconds.

## 4. High Concept Pitch ("X for Y")

Places the startup in a known reference frame using an established company as shorthand.

**Formula:**
```
[Known company/product] for [new market/audience].
```

**Rules:**
- The reference company's business model must be instantly understood
- The new market must be obviously different from the reference's market
- Use at early stage for clarity; retire as you scale
- This is a secondary format — use alongside the one-liner, not instead of it

**When it works:** The analogy clicks instantly and the audience nods.

**When it fails:** The analogy is a stretch, or makes the company seem derivative. YC warns against over-reliance on this format.

## 5. One-Word Pitch

A single word that encapsulates the brand's core identity. From Daniel Pink's *To Sell Is Human*.

**The discipline:** Answer "what is the ONE thing we stand for?"

**Examples:**
- Obama: "Change"
- Google: "Search"
- FedEx: "Overnight"

**When to use:** Brand identity exercises, tagline development, forcing functions for clarity. Rarely used in investor contexts — more useful as an internal alignment tool.

## 6. Elevator Pitch (5-7 Sentences, 30-60 Seconds)

The classic verbal pitch for spontaneous encounters. Long enough to establish context, short enough to maintain attention without slides.

**Structure:**
```
1. Hook — [surprising stat, provocative question, or the one-liner]
2. Problem — [who suffers, how badly, what they do today]
3. Solution — [what you built, how it works in user terms]
4. Why you / unique insight — [what you know that others don't]
5. Traction / proof — [users, revenue, growth, pilots]
6. Market opportunity — [how big this gets]
7. Ask / CTA — [what you want from this conversation]
```

**Variations by length:**
- **30 seconds:** One-liner + problem + solution + ask (sentences 1, 2, 3, 7)
- **60 seconds:** Full 7-part version above
- **2 minutes:** Expand each section with one additional supporting detail

**Testing:** Record yourself delivering it. If it takes longer than 60 seconds at conversational pace, cut.

**When to use:** Conferences, networking events, warm intros, demo nights, hallway conversations.

## 7. Geoffrey Moore Positioning Statement

From *Crossing the Chasm*. The most-cited positioning template in startup and marketing contexts. Designed as an internal thinking tool — use it to sharpen your positioning, then adapt the language for spoken delivery.

**The 6-Line Template:**
```
For [target customers]
who are dissatisfied with [the current market alternative],
our product is a [new product category]
that provides [key problem-solving capability].
Unlike [the product alternative],
our product [primary differentiation].
```

**Condensed 2-Sentence Version:**
```
For [target customer] who [statement of need],
[product name] is a [product category] that [key benefit].
Unlike [competitive alternative], our product [primary differentiation].
```

**Filling the blanks — what each field actually asks:**

| Field | What It Really Asks | Common Mistake |
|-------|-------------------|----------------|
| Target customers | Who has the most urgent version of this problem? | Writing "SMBs" instead of "plumbing companies with 5-20 employees" |
| Current alternative | What do they actually use today? (including "nothing" or "spreadsheets") | Naming a competitor instead of the actual workflow |
| New product category | What mental shelf does this go on? | Using jargon or inventing a category no one recognizes |
| Key capability | What ONE thing does it solve? | Listing 5 capabilities instead of the primary one |
| Product alternative | The closest direct competitor or substitute | Picking a weak competitor to look good — pick the strongest one |
| Primary differentiation | What do you do that the alternative CAN'T? | Listing features instead of the strategic difference |

**When to use:** Internal alignment, marketing briefs, website copy foundations, pitch preparation (not direct delivery to investors — adapt the language).

## 8. Pixar Pitch (6-Sentence Story)

From Daniel Pink's *To Sell Is Human*, adapted from Pixar's story spine. Turns the pitch into a narrative arc.

**Template:**
```
Once upon a time, _____.          → [Your target customer and their world]
Every day, _____.                 → [The problem / status quo pain]
Until one day, _____.             → [Your solution enters]
Because of that, _____.           → [The primary benefit / value delivered]
Because of that, _____.           → [The compounding effect / traction / proof]
Until finally, _____.             → [The vision / market opportunity]
```

**When to use:** Story-driven contexts — blog posts, press pitches, conference talks, internal narratives. Works particularly well for the "Personal Problem" narrative arc.

**When NOT to use:** Cold emails to investors (too long, too narrative). Use the 2-sentence or 3-sentence pitch instead.

## 9. Cold Email Pitch (150-200 Words)

The written equivalent of the elevator pitch, optimized for email. Goal: get one reply ("I'd love to learn more"), not close a deal.

**4 Required Components:**

```
1. One-sentence hook tied to the recipient's specific context
2. 2-4 bullet points of traction metrics
3. A trackable deck link (DocSend, Papermark, or similar)
4. A clear ask with 2-3 proposed meeting times
```

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name],

I'm [Your Name], founder of [Company] — [one-liner tied to their thesis/interest].

[One sentence connecting to something specific about them — recent investment,
published interest, mutual connection.]

Key traction:
• [Metric 1: e.g., $45K MRR, growing 20% MoM]
• [Metric 2: e.g., 3 enterprise pilots signed]
• [Metric 3: e.g., team credential or unfair advantage]

I'd love to share our deck and get your perspective.
Available [Day/Time 1], [Day/Time 2], or [Day/Time 3]?

[Your Name]
[Company] | [Email] | [Website]
```

**Adaptation for internal/corporate context:**
Replace investor-specific language with internal framing:
- "I noticed you recently invested in..." → "Given the [company]'s focus on [strategic priority]..."
- Traction bullets → pilot results, internal champion endorsements, cost savings data
- Deck link → meeting request with one-pager attached
- Meeting times → specific calendar block suggestion

**Rules:**
- Under 200 words — every word earns its place
- Personalize the first line to the specific recipient
- Goal is "a reply, not a check"
- No attachments in cold emails — use trackable links

## 10. One-Pager / Executive Summary

A condensed written overview of the entire business on a single page. Sent before meetings to qualify interest, or used as a leave-behind.

**Sections:**
```
1. Company logo + one-liner                    (header)
2. Team — photos, names, relevant backgrounds  (2-3 lines)
3. Pain point — the problem, stated bluntly    (2-3 sentences)
4. Product — what you built                    (2-3 sentences + screenshot if available)
5. Market size — TAM and SAM                   (1-2 lines with numbers)
6. Revenue model — how you make money          (1-2 sentences)
7. Traction — growth chart or key metrics      (visual if possible)
8. The ask — dollar amount or resource request  (1 line)
9. Contact information                         (footer)
```

**Key distinctions:**
- Text-forward document (not slides)
- One page maximum — if it's two pages, it's not a one-pager
- Should work as a standalone document — reader shouldn't need additional context
- For internal pitches: replace "The Ask" with "Resource Request" and add a "Strategic Alignment" bullet

**When to use:**
- Sent to investors before agreeing to meet (replaces a cold deck send)
- Leave-behind after a meeting
- Accelerator applications
- Conference networking follow-up
- Internal: pre-read before stakeholder meetings

## Daniel Pink's 6 Pitch Types

These are alternative pitch *modes*, not replacements for the formats above. Use them as techniques within other formats.

### The Question Pitch
```
[Reframe your core value proposition as a question]
```
Example: "What would your business look like if you never had to hire a back-office admin?"
**Why it works:** Questions prompt self-persuasion; statements create resistance.

### The Rhyming Pitch
```
[Statement that rhymes — memorable enough to repeat]
```
**Rule:** Don't force it. Rhymes that feel manufactured backfire. Only use if it comes naturally.

### The Subject-Line Pitch
```
[Number] ways to [solution / outcome]
```
Example: "3 ways to cut your back-office costs by 90%"
**Two modes:** Utility (what the reader gains) or Curiosity (creates information gap).

### The Twitter Pitch
```
[Core pitch in under 140 characters — limit to 120 for shareability]
```
**Why the constraint matters:** If you can't say it in 140 characters, you haven't refined it enough.
