# Corporate / Internal Innovation Pitch Reference

Specific guidance for pitching within a corporation — incubators, innovation labs, corporate venture. Load this file when the user's context is internal rather than external VC.

---

## Why Corporate Pitches Are Fundamentally Different

External VC pitching is a distributed betting model: investors make many bets expecting 90% failure rates. Internal corporate pitching is a single-gate approval system: one governance board, one shot, and the default is rejection because the "opportunity cost" is protecting existing business.

**The implication:** A VC evaluates "could this be a 100x return?" A corporate exec evaluates "could this embarrass me, consume my team's resources, and distract from quarterly targets?" These are different questions requiring different answers.

---

## The Seven Traps of Internal Ventures

These are the failure modes specific to corporate innovation. Each one has implications for how you pitch.

### 1. The Risk Trap
**What it is:** Corporate expects predictable returns; venture requires tolerating high failure rates.
**Pitch implication:** Frame as a low-risk experiment with defined milestones, not a big bet. Include explicit kill criteria — "if we don't hit X by date Y, we stop."

### 2. The Reward Trap
**What it is:** Judged on immediate profit, not enterprise value. Promising early-stage ventures get killed as "not big enough, soon enough."
**Pitch implication:** Show the 5-year compounding trajectory, but anchor on 90-day deliverables. The ask should be for a pilot, not a business unit.

### 3. The Time Trap
**What it is:** Leadership focused on quarterly earnings; game-changing businesses compound slowly then break out.
**Pitch implication:** Map your milestones to quarterly review cadences. Every quarter should have a visible proof point.

### 4. The Talent Trap
**What it is:** High performers won't leave secure positions for risky internal ventures.
**Pitch implication:** Address team capability honestly. Show how existing skills transfer and what gaps you'll fill. Don't pretend 2 people can do the work of 10.

### 5. The Supply Trap
**What it is:** Ideas sourced from hackathons and suggestion boxes instead of systematic deal flow.
**Pitch implication:** If your idea came from a systematic process (customer research, market analysis, competitive intelligence), say so. It signals rigor over random inspiration.

### 6. The Demand Trap
**What it is:** One shot at one governance board vs. a VC's portfolio approach.
**Pitch implication:** De-risk the ask. Request the minimum viable budget. Multiple funding scenarios (minimal/target/stretch) give the board options beyond binary yes/no.

### 7. The "Going It Alone" Trap
**What it is:** No external R&D scaffolding — the internal venture operates without the ecosystem support that external startups have.
**Pitch implication:** Identify external partnerships, open-source tools, or platform leverage that reduces build cost. Show you're not reinventing everything.

## Corporate Antibodies

Internal pitches often succeed at the executive level and die in middle management. Middle managers have career stakes in existing systems and become blocking forces after initial approval.

**The pattern:**
1. Exec says "this is exciting, approved"
2. Implementation requires cooperation from department X
3. Department X manager sees this as a threat to their budget/headcount/relevance
4. Death by a thousand small blockers: delayed access, deprioritized integrations, withheld data

**Counter-strategies for the pitch:**

- **Enroll key middle managers as champions BEFORE the formal pitch.** Don't surprise them in the meeting — brief them privately first.
- **Name the integration dependencies in the deck.** "This requires access to [system] owned by [team]." Unnamed dependencies become invisible blockers.
- **Include a stakeholder map** showing who benefits and who might resist. Executives appreciate political awareness — it signals you can navigate the organization, not just build the product.
- **Propose a "coalition of the willing"** — start with teams that WANT this, not teams that you need to convince. Early wins create internal momentum.

## The Executive Sponsor Dependency

Unlike VC pitches where the investor IS the decision maker, internal pitches require a senior executive who will stake their credibility on the project.

**Without a sponsor:**
- Budget gets reallocated at the next review cycle
- Competing priorities consume team attention
- No one advocates for the project when you're not in the room

**In the pitch:**
- If you have a sponsor, name them and describe their commitment level
- If you don't, make "securing an executive sponsor" an explicit Phase 1 milestone
- Frame the sponsor relationship as risk mitigation, not politics: "Executive sponsorship from [name] ensures sustained resource commitment through the pilot phase"

## Innovation Theater Warning

Many corporate innovation programs perform the rituals of innovation (hackathons, incubators, accelerator programs) without actual resource commitment or risk tolerance.

**Red flags you're in innovation theater:**
- No real budget allocated — "use existing headcount"
- No executive sponsor with P&L authority
- Success criteria are activity metrics (demos given, prototypes built) not outcome metrics (revenue, users, retention)
- The "incubator" reports to marketing or HR, not to a business unit
- Winners of the pitch competition get congratulations but no resources

**If you're in this situation:**
- Don't pretend it's a real venture pitch. Calibrate expectations.
- Focus on getting a single pilot with a real business unit — this generates real data that can break through the theater.
- The pitch goal shifts from "get funded" to "get permission to prove it works with real customers."

## The McKinney 5/5/30 Framework

Designed specifically for corporate innovation pitches. Three independent layers — each must work on its own.

### 5-Second Pitch (~10 words)
**Purpose:** The hook that earns the next 5 minutes.
**Rule:** This is NOT an abbreviation of the 5-minute version. It's a standalone hook.
**Method:** Instead of explaining the solution, state the outcome in the simplest possible terms.

Example: "It makes your back-office run itself" instead of "An AI-powered automation platform that handles scheduling, invoicing, and customer communication for trades businesses."

### 5-Minute Pitch
**Purpose:** Framework for the problem, solution, and why/how — enough to decide if the 30-minute is worth it.
**Structure:**
```
1. The hook (from 5-second pitch) — 15 seconds
2. The problem — who has it, how bad it is, what it costs — 60 seconds
3. The solution — what you built, how it works — 90 seconds
4. Why now — what changed that makes this possible — 30 seconds
5. Proof — traction, pilot results, customer quotes — 60 seconds
6. The ask — what you need to continue — 15 seconds
```

### 30-Minute Pitch
**Purpose:** Full presentation using the corporate innovation deck framework (15-18 slides).
**Key:** This is the only level that uses slides. The 5-second and 5-minute versions are purely verbal.

## Language Translation Guide

The same concept needs different language for different corporate audiences:

| Concept | VC Language | Corporate Executive Language |
|---------|------------|---------------------------|
| Market opportunity | "$50B TAM" | "Addresses a gap in our service offering that competitors are filling" |
| Traction | "Growing 30% MoM" | "Pilot with [business unit] reduced processing time by 70%" |
| Competitive advantage | "First mover in $XB market" | "Leverages our existing customer relationships and data" |
| Team | "2x founders, previously exited" | "Cross-functional team with deep domain expertise in [company's core business]" |
| The ask | "$2M seed round at $10M cap" | "Q3 budget allocation of $250K for 6-month pilot with defined success criteria" |
| Risk | "This could be huge" | "Contained pilot with clear kill criteria — if X doesn't happen by Y, we stop" |
| Why us | "No one else has this insight" | "We're uniquely positioned because of [existing company asset/relationship/data]" |
| Exit / outcome | "IPO or acquisition" | "New revenue line / cost reduction / strategic capability / competitive moat" |

## Internal Pitch Deck Adaptations

When using any standard deck framework for an internal audience, make these substitutions:

| Standard Slide | Internal Adaptation |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Title | Add department/division + sponsor name |
| Problem | Frame as business impact to the company specifically |
| Team | Headcount plan with roles and hiring timeline, not founder bios |
| Competition | Include "do nothing" as a competitor + internal alternatives |
| Financial Projections | Multiple scenarios (minimal/target/stretch) |
| The Ask | Resource request with quarterly milestones, not a round |
| — | **Add: Strategic Fit** (slide 2 — immediately after exec summary) |
| — | **Add: Research Results** (mandatory validation evidence) |
| — | **Add: Roadmap** (product development timeline) |
| — | **Add: Risk Mitigation** (what if this fails?) |
| — | **Add: Stakeholder Map** (who benefits, who's involved) |

## Stage-Gate Adaptation

If the corporate innovation program uses a stage-gate process, the pitch evolves at each gate:

| Gate | Pitch Scope | Slides | Focus |
|------|------------|--------|-------|
| Gate 0: Ideation | Concept pitch | 3-5 | Problem hypothesis + why it matters to the company |
| Gate 1: Validation | Problem-solution fit | 8-10 | Customer evidence + early prototype + resource ask for pilot |
| Gate 2: Pilot | Pilot results | 12-15 | Pilot data + unit economics + scale plan |
| Gate 3: Scale | Full business case | 15-18 | Full corporate innovation deck with financials and roadmap |

**The Strategic Fit requirement stays constant across all gates.** Even at Gate 0, the connection to corporate strategy must be clear.
