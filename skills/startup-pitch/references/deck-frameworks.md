# Pitch Deck Frameworks Reference

Slide-by-slide structures for each major deck framework. Load this file when the user is ready for Phase 3 deck creation.

## Framework Selection Guide

| Framework | Best For | Slides | Distinctive Feature |
|-----------|----------|--------|-------------------|
| **Kawasaki 10/20/30** | Classic VC meetings, Series A+ | 10 | "Underlying Magic" slide forces demo/proof |
| **Sequoia** | Institutional VCs, strong timing thesis | 10 | "Why Now?" as dedicated slide |
| **YC Seed** | Pre-seed/seed, accelerator context | 6-8 | Traction-forward, minimal slides |
| **Standard P/S/M** | General purpose, any audience | 12 | The universal baseline |
| **Corporate Innovation** | Internal incubators, exec committees | 15-18 | Strategic Fit + Risk Mitigation |
| **a16z Two-Deck** | Sophisticated fundraising process | 10-12 + 20-30 | Separate narrative vs. data decks |

## Guy Kawasaki 10/20/30

**The rule:** 10 slides, 20 minutes, no font smaller than 30pt.

The 30pt font rule is deliberate — it forces distillation. If you can't say it in 30pt type, you haven't thought it through.

| # | Slide | What Goes On It | Common Mistake |
|---|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | **Title** | Company name, tagline, your name, contact info | Cluttering with logos and investor names |
| 2 | **Problem / Opportunity** | The pain point — make the audience feel it | Describing the problem abstractly instead of viscerally |
| 3 | **Value Proposition** | Your unique solution and why it endures | Listing features instead of the core value |
| 4 | **Underlying Magic** | Technology, methodology, or "secret sauce" — DEMO, screenshots, proof | Claiming magic without showing it |
| 5 | **Business Model** | How you make money; revenue streams | Showing 5 revenue streams instead of the primary one |
| 6 | **Go-to-Market Plan** | How you reach customers; acquisition channels; sequence | Listing channels without sequencing or proof |
| 7 | **Competitive Analysis** | Who else is doing this; your differentiation | Feature checkbox matrix; pick the strongest competitor instead |
| 8 | **Management Team** | Who you are and why you can execute | Generic bios; show relevant accomplishments only |
| 9 | **Financial Projections** | 3-year forecast; unit economics; burn rate | Hockey-stick graphs with no assumptions shown |
| 10 | **Current Status + Use of Funds** | What you've done; what you need the money for | Vague "marketing and engineering" — be specific |

**Kawasaki's emphasis:** Slide 4 (Underlying Magic) separates this from generic templates. It forces you to SHOW, not just claim. If you can't demo it, show a screenshot. If you can't screenshot it, describe the mechanism in concrete terms.

## Sequoia Capital

From Sequoia's "Writing a Business Plan" guide. More narrative than Kawasaki — emphasizes company thinking, not just facts.

| # | Slide | What Goes On It | Sequoia's Guidance |
|---|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Company Purpose** | One declarative sentence defining the company | "Define your company in a single declarative sentence. This is harder than it looks." |
| 2 | **Problem** | Customer pain + how they solve it today (without you) | Show the gap between what exists and what should exist |
| 3 | **Solution** | Your product's unique value; what makes it durable | Not features — the strategic advantage |
| 4 | **Why Now?** | Market forces, technology shifts, or regulatory changes | "Why hasn't this been built before?" — the hardest and most important slide |
| 5 | **Market Size** | TAM/SAM/SOM; bottom-up calculation preferred | Show your math; top-down projections are ignored |
| 6 | **Competition** | Competitive landscape + winning strategy | Direct + indirect competitors; explain WHY you win, not just THAT you win |
| 7 | **Product** | What it looks like; how it works in practice | Screenshots, user flow, demo — make it concrete |
| 8 | **Business Model** | Revenue model + unit economics | Path to sustainability, not just revenue |
| 9 | **Team** | Why this team can execute; relevant experience | Unfair advantages for this specific problem |
| 10 | **Financials** | Multi-year revenue projections | Assumptions matter more than numbers |

**Sequoia's distinctive emphasis:**
- "Why Now?" is the hardest slide. Most founders punt with "AI is trending" or "market is growing." Force a specific mechanism: what changed in the last 12-24 months?
- Company Purpose must be a single declarative sentence, not a mission statement
- Ends on Financials rather than an ask — Sequoia assumes if the numbers work, they'll reach out
- Less is more — clarity of thinking matters more than production value

## YC Seed Deck (Michael Seibel)

YC's own guidance for seed-stage decks. Traction-forward — investors at seed stage fund momentum, not projections.

| # | Element | What Goes On It | Seibel's Guidance |
|---|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **What You Do** | 2 sentences + one specific example | "80% accurate, 100% understandable beats the reverse" |
| 2 | **Team** | Specific accomplishments, not titles | Show unfair advantages for executing this vision |
| 3 | **Traction** | Revenue, growth rate, user numbers with timeframes | "Investors fund momentum, not absolute numbers" |
| 4 | **Unique Insight** | Non-obvious knowledge about the problem/solution | What do big players in your market not understand? |
| 5 | **Market Size** | Bottom-up calculation, show your math | Transparent methodology > big numbers |
| 6 | **The Ask** | Funding amount + specific milestones + 18-24 month timeline | Explicit — don't make investors guess |

**YC Demo Day variant** (2-2.5 minutes, live audience of ~1,500):

| # | Slide | Time |
|---|-------|------|
| 1 | Intro + tagline | 10-15 sec |
| 2 | Problem | 15-20 sec |
| 3 | Solution + demo/screenshot | 20-30 sec |
| 4 | Traction | 20-30 sec |
| 5 | Market size | 15 sec |
| 6 | Team | 10-15 sec |
| 7 | Summary + ask | 10 sec |

**YC design philosophy:**
- "Make investors talk at least 50% of the time" — the deck is a conversation starter, not a monologue
- Visually boring slides with large clear text beat polished slides with small text
- Traction is the most important signal at seed — lead with it after intro
- The goal is to get investors to seek you out for a 1:1 — the pitch is a filter, not a close

## Standard Problem/Solution/Market (12 Slides)

The universal baseline. The skeleton inside virtually every investor deck. Maps to a three-act story: *here's the world today (problem) → here's what changes (solution) → here's how big this gets (market)*.

| # | Slide | Act | What Goes On It |
|---|-------|-----|-----------------|
| 1 | **Cover** | — | Company name + one-sentence description |
| 2 | **Problem** | Act 1 | The pain point; who suffers from it |
| 3 | **Solution** | Act 1 | How you solve it |
| 4 | **Market Size** | Act 2 | TAM / SAM / SOM |
| 5 | **Product** | Act 2 | Screenshots, demo, how it works |
| 6 | **Traction** | Act 2 | Revenue, users, growth, key milestones |
| 7 | **Business Model** | Act 2 | How you make money |
| 8 | **Go-to-Market** | Act 2 | How you acquire customers |
| 9 | **Competition** | Act 3 | Competitive landscape + differentiation |
| 10 | **Team** | Act 3 | Who you are and why you win |
| 11 | **Financials** | Act 3 | Projections + key metrics |
| 12 | **The Ask** | Act 3 | Amount, use of funds, milestones |

## Corporate Innovation Deck (15-18 Slides)

For internal incubators, innovation labs, and corporate venture pitches. Key differences from startup decks: denser content expected, mandatory strategic alignment, headcount planning instead of founder bios, multiple funding scenarios.

| # | Slide | Corporate-Specific Notes |
|---|-------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Executive Summary** | Dense overview for exec pre-read — this IS read without you presenting |
| 2 | **Strategic Fit** | **THE most critical slide.** How this aligns with corporate strategy. Can compensate for weak financials. Must answer: "Why should [company] do this vs. a startup?" |
| 3 | **Problem / Opportunity** | Frame in language leadership cares about — revenue impact, customer retention, competitive threat |
| 4 | **Solution / Value Proposition** | What you're building and why it works |
| 5 | **Product** | Screenshots, demo, mockups |
| 6 | **Why Now?** | Market timing + why the company is positioned to act now |
| 7 | **Research Results** | **Mandatory.** Customer validation evidence — interviews, pilots, data. Corporate execs won't bet on intuition |
| 8 | **Business Model** | Revenue approach + path to sustainability |
| 9 | **Unit Economics** | More granular than startup decks expect |
| 10 | **Market Size** | TAM/SAM/SOM with bottom-up calculation |
| 11 | **Competition** | Competitive landscape — include internal alternatives and "do nothing" |
| 12 | **Roadmap** | **Required.** Product development timeline with milestones |
| 13 | **Go-to-Market Plan** | How you reach customers leveraging company assets |
| 14 | **Team / Capability** | Headcount, roles, hiring timeline — not founder stories |
| 15 | **Financials + Key Metrics** | Multi-year projections with assumptions |
| 16 | **Investment Required** | **Multiple scenarios:** minimal / target / stretch. Corporate budgeting expects options |
| 17 | **Risk Mitigation** | Anticipated challenges + contingency plans — address "what if this fails?" proactively |
| 18 | **Appendix** | Supporting data, detailed financials, technical architecture |

**Corporate deck design principles:**
- Denser text is acceptable — corporate audiences expect pre-reads and reference material
- Strategic Fit slide is the "IRR equivalent" — if this doesn't land, nothing else matters
- Multiple funding scenarios signal maturity and reduce perceived risk
- Include "do nothing" as a competitive alternative — what happens if the company doesn't pursue this?
- Risk mitigation slide is not optional — execs approve low-risk experiments, not big bets

## a16z Two-Deck Strategy

Andreessen Horowitz explicitly recommends building two versions for different jobs:

### Narrative Deck (10-12 slides)
**For:** Live presentations, first meetings
**Tone:** Story-forward, emotional, memorable
**Content:** Problem → solution → why now → market → team → vision
**Design:** Large type, one idea per slide, product visuals
**What stays OUT:** Detailed unit economics, CAC/LTV breakdowns, financial model

### Data Deck (20-30 slides)
**For:** Due diligence, partner meetings, follow-up review
**Tone:** Evidence-forward, comprehensive, referenceable
**Content:** Everything in the narrative deck PLUS:
- Detailed financials and unit economics
- Cohort analyses and retention curves
- Competitive deep-dive with market maps
- Technical architecture and IP overview
- Customer testimonials and case studies
- Cap table and funding history

**The principle:** The live pitch and the diligence process are different jobs requiring different documents. Cramming diligence data into a narrative deck ruins both.

## Airbnb's Original Deck (Archetype)

Often cited as the cleanest example of problem/solution/market narrative. Notable for leading with market validation before claiming market size.

| # | Slide | Why It's Notable |
|---|-------|-----------------|
| 1 | Welcome / Cover | "Book rooms with locals, rather than hotels" — tagline IS the cover |
| 2 | Problem | Price + disconnection from local culture — dual pain |
| 3 | Solution | Cheaper + culturally immersive — mirrors the dual pain |
| 4 | **Market Validation** | Craigslist + Couchsurfing data as proof demand exists |
| 5 | Market Size | 84M trips projected; revenue potential |
| 6 | Product | User flow screenshots |
| 7 | Business Model | 10% commission; $2.1B revenue projection |
| 8 | Adoption Strategy | Events, partnerships, Craigslist cross-posting |
| 9 | Competition | "Cheapest online solution" — one clear positioning claim |
| 10 | Competitive Advantages | 6 differentiators listed |
| 11 | Team | Founding team bios |
| 12 | Press | Media validation |
| 13 | User Testimonials | Social proof |
| 14 | Financial / The Ask | $500K to reach $2M yearly revenue |

**The key innovation:** Slide 4 (Market Validation) before Slide 5 (Market Size). They proved demand existed before claiming how big it could get. This is a bottom-up credibility move — show evidence, then project from it.
